Essere e stare: due verbi per "to be"

English has one to be. Italian has two: essere and stare. The split is the conceptual hurdle an English speaker hits in the first weeks of Italian, and it’s the place where a French speaker’s instincts can lead them slightly astray — French has only one être, so the French brain wants to reach for essere every time. Spanish speakers have the closest analogue with ser / estar, but the Italian split doesn’t line up exactly with Spanish, and a few Italian uses would feel "wrong" to a strictly Spanish ear.

Il presente di essere

Italian’s primary "to be" verb. The conjugation is short and mostly regular, but one form does something worth flagging immediately.

PersonFormExample
iosonosono italiano — I’m Italian
tuseisei stanca — you’re tired (f)
lui / leièè romano — he’s Roman
noisiamosiamo in Italia — we’re in Italy
voisietesiete qui — you’re here (pl)
lorosonosono ricchi — they’re rich

The sono syncretism

Notice that sono appears twice — once for 1st person singular (io sono, "I am") and once for 3rd person plural (loro sono, "they are"). Same form, different subjects. Italian is unusual among Romance languages for collapsing these two into one form:

ItalianFrenchSpanish
I amsonosuissoy
they aresonosontson

Context always disambiguates. If the subject is visible, agreement is automatic: io sono is 1sg, loro sono is 3pl. If the subject is dropped — Italian commonly omits subject pronouns when they’re obvious — the rest of the sentence gives you the clue: sono italiano with a singular masculine adjective is 1sg ("I am Italian"); sono italiani with a plural adjective is 3pl ("they are Italian"). Read the agreement, pick the person.

This is the headline case, but worth knowing that it only bites on essere. For most other verbs Italian distinguishes 1sg and 3pl clearly — ho / hanno, faccio / fanno, dico / dicono.

Il presente di stare

Italian’s second "to be," used for states, some locations, fixed expressions, and the progressive construction that we’ll meet below.

PersonFormExample
iostosto bene — I’m well
tustaistai attento — be careful
lui / leistasta per piovere — it’s about to rain
noistiamostiamo bene — we’re fine
voistatestate zitti — be quiet (pl)
lorostannostanno mangiando — they’re eating

Although stare looks like a regular -are verb, it’s irregular: stai, sta, and stanno don’t follow the regular pattern (which would give *stai, *sta, *stano). Memorize these forms together with fare and dare, which share the same irregularity (fai / fa / fanno, dai / dà / danno).

When to use which

Essere is the default. Reach for it first, and you’ll be right most of the time. Italian uses essere for:

  • Identity: sono Maria — I’m Maria.
  • Origin and nationality: è italiano — he’s Italian.
  • Profession: è un medico — she’s a doctor.
  • Stable characteristics: è alta — she’s tall.
  • Feelings and emotions: sono felice — I’m happy.
  • Time and dates: sono le tre — it’s three o’clock.
  • Location of people and things in neutral register: siamo a Roma — we’re in Rome.

Stare is more specialized. Use it for:

  • Health and state of being: sto bene, sta male — I’m well, he’s unwell.
  • The progressive (stare + gerund): sto mangiando, stanno parlando — I’m eating, they’re speaking.
  • Stare per + infinitive, meaning "about to": sta per partire — he’s about to leave.
  • Fixed expressions: stare attento (to pay attention), stare zitto (to keep quiet), stare tranquillo (to stay calm).
  • Location, especially with durative or volitional sense: sto a casa can mean "I’m at home" (colloquial, more common in the south) or, more broadly, "I’m staying in / I’m staying home [for the duration]." Standard Italian uses sono a casa for neutral location without the stay-put connotation.

Stare + gerundio: the progressive

This is the main stare construction a learner meets early, and it’s worth getting comfortable with quickly. Stare + gerundio marks an action in progress right now — sto mangiando means "I’m in the act of eating at this moment," more emphatic than the plain present mangio (which can also cover right-now but is unmarked for aspect).

The gerundio is formed from the infinitive. For -are verbs, drop -are and add -ando: mangiare → mangiando, parlare → parlando. For -ere and -ire verbs, add -endo: leggere → leggendo, dormire → dormendo. The combined construction always uses a conjugated stare plus the invariant gerund: stai leggendo, stiamo cucinando, stanno arrivando.

Notes for Spanish speakers

Italian essere / stare mirrors Spanish ser / estar, and the parallel holds for most core uses — but the dividing line sits slightly differently. Two particular places Italian diverges from Spanish:

  • Location of permanent things. Spanish la torre está en Pisa uses estar; Italian la torre è a Pisa uses essere. Italian uses essere for location more freely than Spanish does.
  • Feelings and moods. Spanish estoy cansado uses estar for a temporary feeling; Italian sono stanco uses essere. The "temporary vs permanent" distinction Spanish insists on is blurred in Italian.

Put another way: in Spanish, estar covers a lot of ground; in Italian, essere covers that ground plus more. If in doubt, reach for essere. You’ll be right most of the time, and the cases where you need stare are a short list that you can learn as fixed patterns — health and state, the progressive, stare per + infinitive, and a handful of set expressions.

Preview: essere as auxiliary verb

One role of essere that you’ll meet repeatedly before the next grammar lesson explains it: essere is the auxiliary verb for the passato prossimo of certain Italian verbs — especially intransitive verbs of motion and state change, plus all reflexive verbs. Sentences like sono arrivato (I’ve arrived), è diventato (he became), siamo partiti (we left), si è alzata (she got up) all use essere as the auxiliary. Avere serves as auxiliary for transitive verbs — the topic of the next lesson.
