Avere: possession, auxiliary, idioms

Italian avere (to have) is one of the two most-used verbs in the language, alongside essere. It does three jobs, each worth understanding in its own right: it marks possession, it serves as the auxiliary verb for the passato prossimo of transitive verbs, and it forms a large family of fixed idioms where the Italian pattern is "avere + noun" where English uses "be + adjective." French speakers have a significant head start here, because the French verb avoir works the same way in almost every respect.

The silent h

The initial h in ho, hai, ha, and hanno is silent. It exists purely for writing — Italian has no aspirated /h/ sound anywhere in the language. But the h is not optional; it carries a specific orthographic job. It distinguishes the verb forms from unrelated words that sound identical:

With h (verb)Without hSame sound
ho — I haveo — or/o/
hai — you haveai — to the (m pl)/ai/
ha — he/she hasa — to, at/a/
hanno — they haveanno — year/ˈan.no/

A phrase like hanno un anno ("they are one year old," literally "they have one year") sounds identical twice over: /ˈan.no un ˈan.no/. The spelling is what keeps the verb and the noun distinct on the page. Italians don’t pronounce the h as a puff of breath the way English speakers might — they just write it and silently pass over it. French does something similar in spots (compare French a "has" vs à "to", kept apart by an accent rather than an h), but the Italian convention is specifically to write an h.

Possession

The simplest use of avere: to say someone has something. The syntax matches English to have and French avoir exactly — subject + avere + direct object.

  • ho una casa — I have a house
  • hai i biglietti — you have the tickets
  • abbiamo un cane — we have a dog
  • hanno molti libri — they have many books

Avere as auxiliary: il passato prossimo

Italian’s everyday past tense — the passato prossimo, equivalent to English "I have eaten" / "I ate" or French j’ai mangé — is built from an auxiliary verb plus the past participle of the main verb. Italian uses two auxiliaries, and the choice between them is one of the first "big" rules an A1 learner has to absorb.

  • Avere is the auxiliary for transitive verbs (verbs that take a direct object). This is the majority case.
  • Essere is the auxiliary for intransitive verbs of motion and state change, and for all reflexive verbs.

For now, focus on the avere pattern. The formula is avere conjugated + past participle:

  • ho mangiato — I ate / I have eaten
  • hai letto — you read / you have read
  • abbiamo finito — we finished / we have finished
  • hanno comprato — they bought / they have bought

Past participles of regular verbs are predictable: -are verbs end in -ato (mangiato, parlato, comprato), -ere verbs end in -uto (creduto, venduto — many are irregular though, like letto, scritto, visto), -ire verbs end in -ito (finito, dormito, sentito).

With avere as auxiliary, the past participle does not agree with the subject — it stays in the neutral -ato / -uto / -ito form regardless of who the subject is. This matches French (j’ai mangé, nous avons mangé — the participle doesn’t agree unless a direct object precedes). With essere as auxiliary the participle does agree with the subject — that’s the major piece of the next lesson.

Avere + noun idioms

The trickiest part of avere for English speakers: a large family of fixed expressions where Italian says "avere + noun" and English says "be + adjective." The grammar of these idioms doesn’t translate word-for-word; you have to learn them as set pieces.

French speakers will find the list deeply familiar — French has the same "avoir + noun" pattern for almost all of these, with only occasional divergences (French uses être pressé for "to be in a hurry" where Italian uses avere fretta). English speakers have to learn it as a new category and suppress the translation instinct: don’t say sono affamato when you mean "I’m hungry" — say ho fame. Sono affamato is grammatical, but it reads as more literary or emphatic ("I am famished"), not as the neutral everyday statement.

Age

One special case of the avere-idiom pattern, important enough to spell out: Italian expresses age with avere, not essere. You don’t be a certain age — you have a certain number of years.

  • ho vent’anni — I’m twenty (years old)
  • ha cinquant’anni — he’s fifty
  • quanti anni hai? — how old are you?
  • mia figlia ha tre anni — my daughter is three

Same pattern in French: j’ai vingt ans, quel âge as-tu?. English, German, Dutch, and the Nordic languages are the outliers that use "to be" for age. If you’re coming from French, just let your existing instinct ride — your brain already wants the right verb.

Notes for French speakers

Most of what’s in this lesson you already have from French, and the mapping is unusually tight. The present-tense conjugation lines up form-for-form: j’ai, tu as, il a, nous avons, vous avez, ils ont ↔ ho, hai, ha, abbiamo, avete, hanno. The auxiliary role in compound tenses is identical (j’ai mangé ↔ ho mangiato). Most of the "avoir + noun" idioms translate one-to-one. Age works the same way.

The one piece that is genuinely new is the silent h that Italian writes at the start of ho / hai / ha / hanno and French doesn’t write at all (ai, not *hai). The Italian h is purely orthographic — it’s the marker that saves the verb from collapsing visually with o, ai, a, and anno. You pronounce all four verb forms exactly as if the h weren’t there, but you write it every time.
