Le cinque vocali

Italian has exactly cinque vocali — a, e, i, o, u — and each one has one sound, always. They don't shift with stress the way English vowels do, they don't reduce to schwa in unstressed syllables, and they don't nasalize the way les voyelles nasales do in French. Five sounds, five symbols, no ambiguity. Once you lock them in, you can read any Italian word aloud. The spelling is the pronunciation.

Two of the vowels — e and o — have an open and a closed form in standard Italian (pèsca "peach" with open e vs. pésca "fishing" with closed e), but the distinction is regional, rarely marked in writing, and safe to ignore at A1. Imitate what you hear; the distinction settles in on its own.

a — open, central, unrounded

Italian a is like the a in English father, or like French la patte. Never the a of English cat, never the schwa of English about, never the nasal an of enfant. It stays open and pure no matter where it falls in the word.

Listen: la casa, la mamma, la pasta, la carta, andare, amare, abbastanza, la bambina.

e — mid front, never a schwa

Italian e ranges from the e of French été (closed) to the e of French père (open). Pick either at A1 — both are accepted — but never reduce it to the schwa of English the. An unstressed Italian e is still a full e.

Listen: bene, tre, ecco, la sera, vedere, mentre, certamente.

i — high front, close

Italian i is like the French i in ici or the English ee in see, but shorter and tenser. Never the English i of bit (which is actually a different vowel: /ɪ/).

Listen: sì certo, il cinema, la pizza, bambini, difficile, invece, tipico.

o — mid back, rounded

Italian o ranges from closed (like French côté) to open (like French notre), but never becomes the diphthongized ow of American English go. It's a single rounded vowel held briefly, then released — no glide.

Listen: no grazie, sono, dopo, la moglie, lontano, importante, il momento.

u — high back, rounded

Italian u is the ou of French vous or the oo of English boot — not the French u of lune, which is fronted and rounded. English speakers should also resist shortening it to the u of put.

Listen: tu sei, una, uno, il muro, la luna, sempre su, il futuro.

Minimal pairs to calibrate your ear

A few near-contrasts to sharpen the five positions. Play each row in sequence; listen for the single-vowel shift that changes the word.

  • pala (shovel) · pela (he peels) · pila (battery) · polo (pole) · pula (chaff)
  • mano (hand) · meno (less) · minore (minor) · mondo (world) · muto (mute)

If you can hear each of the five slots clearly — without conflating a with o, e with i, or o with u — you are ready to read Italian aloud.
