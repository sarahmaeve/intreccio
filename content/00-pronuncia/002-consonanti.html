Le consonanti trappola

Italian consonants are mostly predictable — p, b, t, d, f, v, m, n, l all behave the way an English speaker expects. The trouble spots are a short list of spelling conventions that differ from French and English, plus the single biggest stumbling block for any non-Italian: doppie consonanti, the geminated consonants that change word meaning. Lock in the five patterns below and the rest is just vocabulary.

r — tapped, not American

Italian r is a single tap of the tongue against the ridge behind the upper teeth, close to the Spanish single r of pero. In intervocalic position (between two vowels) it’s always a tap; at the beginning of a word, or doubled, it trills. Do not use the American retroflex r or the French uvular r; both sound distinctly foreign and sometimes unintelligible in Italian ears.

Listen: Roma, rosso, raro, il primo, caro amico, per favore, arrivederci, la birra.

gn — the palatal nasal /ɲ/

Italian gn is identical to the French gn of montagne, or Spanish ñ — the palatal nasal /ɲ/. Never pronounce it as /ɡn/ (like English ignore) or as two separate letters. This one is a gift for French speakers and a frequent trip-up for English speakers.

Listen: la montagna, lo gnocco, il bagno, insegnare, la compagnia, il sogno, disegnare.

gl(i) — the palatal lateral /ʎ/

Italian gl followed by i is the palatal lateral /ʎ/, similar but not identical to French ill in fille. It does not sound like the gl of English glad. Before other vowels, gl is the regular consonant cluster /ɡl/ (la gloria), but the gli pattern is by far more common and the one that matters.

Listen: gli studenti, la famiglia, il figlio, la moglie, meglio, voglio, la maglietta, il coniglio.

sc(i) and sc(e) — the sh sound /ʃ/

Before e or i, Italian sc is /ʃ/ — identical to English sh or French ch in chat. Before a, o, u or a consonant, sc is /sk/ (as in la scuola, /ˈskwɔ.la/). The rule mirrors what c does by itself: soft before e/i, hard everywhere else.

Listen (soft, /ʃ/): la scena, scemo, il pesce, sciare, lasciare, il prosciutto.

Listen (hard, /sk/): la scuola, scuro, scrivere, lo schermo.

c, g and their h-rescue

Italian c and g are hard (/k/, /ɡ/) before a, o, u and soft (/tʃ/, /dʒ/) before e, i — the same logic as French and Spanish. To keep a hard sound before e or i, Italian writes ch or gh. That h is silent; it’s a spelling convention, not a sound.

Listen (c hard / c soft): casa · cento · il cinema · che cosa · la chiesa · il bicchiere.

Listen (g hard / g soft): il gatto · il gelato · la giraffa · gli spaghetti · lunghe · ghiaccio.

Le doppie consonanti — the big one

This is the Italian pronunciation rule that most learners underrate and that most Italians immediately hear. A double consonant is held longer than a single one, with a perceptible pause-in-consonant. It is not loud, and it is not reduplicated; it’s a matter of timing. Getting it wrong doesn’t just sound non-native — it can change the word entirely.

Minimal pairs (single · double):

  • sono (I am) · sonno (sleep)
  • casa (house) · cassa (cashbox, till)
  • pena (pain) · penna (pen)
  • nono (ninth) · nonno (grandfather)
  • copia (copy) · coppia (couple, pair)
  • la nota (the note) · la notte (the night)

The rule applies to every consonant except h and q (which uses cq for geminate — acqua is /ˈakːwa/). Practice by exaggerating the hold on the first consonant of the double — la no-TTE, il no-NNO — then pull back until it feels natural. French speakers tend to under-geminate; English speakers tend to skip the distinction entirely.

s and z — voiced or unvoiced

Italian s and z are each voiced in some positions and unvoiced in others, with some regional variation that’s safe to ignore at A1. Three general rules:

  • s at the start of a word before a vowel: always unvoiced /s/ (il sole).
  • s between two vowels: usually voiced /z/ (la casa is /ˈka.za/, not /ˈka.sa/ — this one trips French and English speakers who expect /s/).
  • s followed by a voiced consonant (b, d, g, l, m, n, r, v): voiced /z/ (la sveglia, lo smalto).

For z, the voiced /dz/ and unvoiced /ts/ distinction is not reliably predictable from spelling; both are written z. Listen and imitate.

Listen: la casa (voiced), il sole (unvoiced), il naso (voiced), la pasta (unvoiced), la zia, lo zucchero, la pizza, il prezzo.

That’s the full set of Italian consonant conventions. The pattern underneath every rule is the same one you already know from French and Spanish: e and i soften some consonants and h is a silent spelling marker that holds them hard. Internalize gn, gli, sc(i), the doppie, and the intervocalic s, and you have Italian phonology.
