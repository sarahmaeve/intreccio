Spaghetti alla Carbonara

One of the most famous and most misunderstood recipes in Italian cooking. Authentic carbonara has four ingredients — guanciale, eggs, pecorino romano, black pepper — and nothing else. No cream, no garlic, no onion, no pancetta instead of guanciale. Technique, not ingredients, makes the difference: the sauce is never cooked over heat; it emulsifies with the residual warmth of the pasta.

Ingredients for 4

  • 400 g spaghetti (or rigatoni, equally traditional)
  • 200 g guanciale in one piece
  • 4 large, fresh egg yolks, at room temperature
  • 80 g pecorino romano DOP, finely grated
  • Freshly ground black pepper, generously
  • Coarse salt for the pasta water

A note on ingredients. Guanciale — cured pork jowl — is richer and grainier than pancetta. If you can’t find it, pancetta is an acceptable fallback, but cooked or smoked pancetta is not. The egg yolks must be fresh and at room temperature: cold yolks don’t emulsify. Pecorino romano, aged and sharply salty, tastes completely different from parmigiano; it is not interchangeable.

Preparation

  1. Bring a large pot of water to a rolling boil. When it boils, salt it with about a tablespoon of coarse salt.
  2. Meanwhile, cut the guanciale into strips roughly half a centimeter thick, removing the rind but leaving the fat attached to the lean meat.
  3. Place a wide pan over medium heat, with no added oil. Put the guanciale into the cold pan and let the fat render slowly, allowing the strips to crisp on the outside while staying tender inside. About seven to eight minutes. Turn off the heat and leave the guanciale in its own rendered fat.
  4. In a wide bowl, beat the egg yolks with a fork. Add the grated pecorino and a generous grind of black pepper. Mix until you have a dense, homogeneous paste. If the mixture is too stiff, loosen it with a tablespoon of pasta-cooking water.
  5. When the water boils, drop in the spaghetti and cook it al dente, draining it one minute before the time stated on the package. Before draining, scoop out and reserve a generous cup of cooking water — you will need it for the emulsion.
  6. Drain the pasta and tip it immediately into the pan with the guanciale (the heat must be off). Toss the pasta vigorously in the guanciale fat for thirty seconds.
  7. Take the pan off the burner. Add the yolk, pecorino, and pepper mixture. Stir rapidly with kitchen tongs, adding the hot pasta water a little at a time. The motion must be continuous: the yolks should emulsify with the fat and the water into a glossy, creamy sauce — never a scrambled egg. If the pan is too hot, the yolks cook and the sauce breaks — in which case, start over.
  8. Serve immediately in pre-warmed plates. Finish each portion with a fresh grind of pepper and a dusting of pecorino.

Technical notes

The most common mistake, in Italy as everywhere else, is cooking the sauce. Real carbonara emulsifies with the residual heat of the pasta, not with active cooking. If you see set yolks, pieces of solidified egg, or a grainy texture: the sauce was overheated. The solution is always the same — take the pan off the heat before adding the yolks, and work quickly with hot pasta water to regulate the consistency.

Pasta-cooking water is the second secret ingredient. Salted and starchy, it’s what binds the sauce to the pasta in a stable emulsion. Without it, carbonara comes out dry and lumpy. Use it without stinting but with attention: one tablespoon at a time.

Carbonara does not wait. Eat it within a minute or two of plating: as the pasta cools, the sauce tightens and loses its creaminess. Set everything up in advance, but do the emulsion at the last moment, with your guests already at the table.
