Gli articoli indeterminativi

English has a and an, chosen by the sound of the following word. French has un and une, chosen by gender. Italian combines both criteria — gender and phonological context — and ends up with four forms: un, uno, una, and un'. The rules are a mirror image of the definite article rules you already know.

The four forms, paired with canonical examples

Masculine. Default is un before any consonant or vowel that isn't in the rescue list: un libro, un tavolo, un nonno, un amico, un albero, un uomo. Note that masculine un takes no apostrophe before a vowel — this is the form's one counterintuitive feature for French speakers, who expect un homme to be elided.

Before s + consonant, z, gn, ps, pn, x, or y, Italian switches to uno — the same rescue rule as the definite article lo: uno studente, uno zaino, uno psicologo, uno gnocco, uno sbaglio.

Feminine. Default is una before any consonant: una casa, una bambina, una nonna, una studentessa, una storia. Before a vowel, elide to un' with the apostrophe: un'amica, un'opera, un'ora, un'altra volta.

The masculine / feminine asymmetry before a vowel

This is the rule most learners get wrong, and the one most worth internalizing:

  • Masculine + vowel → un (no apostrophe): un amico, un albero, un uomo.
  • Feminine + vowel → un' (with apostrophe): un'amica, un'opera, un'idea.

The apostrophe itself carries grammatical information — it tells you the noun is feminine. When you see un' in writing, you know immediately that whatever follows is feminine; when you see un followed by a vowel, it's masculine. French doesn't distinguish this way — un homme and une histoire are both elided, but their articles are visibly different words. Italian packs the same information into a single character.

The rule of thumb

For a masculine noun, use the same three-step check from the definite-article lesson and swap the results:

  1. Starts with s + consonant, z, gn, ps, pn? → uno.
  2. Anything else — consonant or vowel? → un (no apostrophe).

For a feminine noun:

  1. Starts with a vowel? → un' (with apostrophe).
  2. Starts with a consonant? → una.

That's it. Four forms, three tests between them, and a useful diagnostic: the apostrophe marks a feminine noun every time.

No plural indefinite article

Italian does not have a plural indefinite article. English "some books" and French des livres both need a substitute in Italian — you use the articolo partitivo (dei libri, degli amici, delle case), drop the article entirely (libri, amici), or use a quantifier (alcuni libri, qualche libro). The partitive is the closest analogue to French des and deserves its own lesson; for now, remember that un / uno / una / un' have no plurals.

Notes for French speakers

The one habit to break: un before a masculine vowel-initial noun never takes an apostrophe, unlike French pronunciation which liaisons un ami as /œ̃.na.mi/ with an audible linking /n/. Italian un amico is pronounced /u.naˈmi.ko/ — the n of un flows phonologically into the vowel the same way, but orthographically the two words stay separate. The feminine un'amica, by contrast, is written as one typographic unit, and the apostrophe signals both the elision and the gender. Learn to expect the apostrophe on the feminine side only.
