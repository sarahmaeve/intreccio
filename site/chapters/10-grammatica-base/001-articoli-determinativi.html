Gli articoli determinativi

English's simple the and French's le / la / l' / les branch into seven forms in Italian. The choice depends on three things: gender, number, and what sound the following word begins with. Gender and number you already have from French; the sound-based rule is the piece that's new.

The seven forms, each with a canonical example

Masculine singular. Default is il, used before most consonants: il libro, il tavolo, il nonno. Before a vowel, elide to l': l'amico, l'albero, l'uomo. Before s + consonant, z, gn, ps, pn, x, or y, use lo: lo studente, lo zaino, lo psicologo, lo gnocco. Think of lo as the "rescue" article — it prevents the awkward consonant cluster you'd otherwise get with il.

Masculine plural. il pluralizes to i: i libri, i tavoli, i nonni. Both lo and l' pluralize to gli: gli studenti, gli amici, gli uomini. So gli is the plural counterpart to the tricky masculine forms — if you can pick lo or l' in the singular, you already know to use gli in the plural.

Feminine singular. Default is la, used before any consonant: la casa, la bambina, la nonna, la studentessa, la storia. Italian doesn't have a special form for s + consonant on the feminine side — la handles everything. Before a vowel, elide to l': l'amica, l'acqua, l'opera.

Feminine plural. Always le, with no phonological variation: le case, le bambine, le nonne, le amiche, le studentesse. The only article with a single form.

One rule of thumb

For any masculine noun, glance at its first letters and ask:

  1. Does it start with a vowel? → l' (singular) · gli (plural).
  2. Does it start with s + consonant, z, gn, ps, pn? → lo · gli.
  3. Anything else? → il · i.

That three-step check gets you right on 99% of masculine nouns. The feminine side is easier: vowel → l' · le; consonant → la · le.

Notes for French speakers

The pattern that in French elides le / la to l' before a vowel works exactly the same way in Italian for both genders — l'amico parallels l'ami, l'amica parallels l'amie. What's genuinely new is the Italian lo / gli series, which French has no equivalent for. French speakers sometimes want to say il studente by analogy with le stylo; resist that, and remember the s + consonant trigger.

One more parallel worth noticing: in French, les serves as the sole plural article regardless of gender. Italian splits plural along gender lines — masculine has i or gli, feminine has le. Your French habit of using one plural form won't carry over; your Spanish memory of los / las will.
