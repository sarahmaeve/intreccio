# Roman Empire — Triglot Weave Sample

Four paragraphs of French/English/Italian triglot weave covering Italian history from the founding of Rome through national unification in 1870. Calibrated for the learner profile defined in `DESIGN.md`: native English, B2 French, A2–B1 Spanish, entering Italian at A2.

Markup follows the convention in `DESIGN.md §5`:

- **English** is unmarked (default).
- **French** is wrapped in `<span lang="fr">…</span>`.
- **Italian** is wrapped in `<span lang="it">…</span>`.
- **Proper names** that are internationally stable (Rome, Napoleon, Leonardo da Vinci) are left unmarked regardless of which language they "belong" to.

When rendered as HTML with the project stylesheet, French spans appear in deep blue italic and Italian spans in deep green bold italic. In a plain Markdown viewer, the spans render transparently and the text reads as a mostly-English passage with visible foreign words — which is itself a useful "pure reading" view.

## Lesson 001 — The Roman Empire

**Level:** A1 → A2 (ramping)
**Italian share:** paragraph 1 ≈ 15%, paragraph 2 ≈ 30%
**Cognate patterns showcased:** `fr -oire / it -oria`, `fr -té / it -tà`, `fr -tion / it -zione`, `fr -eur / it -ore`
**Grammar features:** infinitives only (no conjugated Italian verbs); fixed Italian phrases; Italian nouns with definite articles to seed gender.

### Paragraph 1 — The founding of Rome

The <span lang="fr">histoire</span> of <span lang="fr">l'Empire romain</span> begins with a small <span lang="fr">ville</span> on the banks of <span lang="fr">le fleuve</span> Tibre — a <span lang="fr">ville</span> the <span lang="fr">Romains</span> called <span lang="it">Roma</span>. According to <span lang="fr">la légende</span>, it was founded <span lang="fr">en</span> 753 <span lang="fr">avant Jésus-Christ</span> by two <span lang="fr">frères</span>, Romulus and Remus. For <span lang="fr">plusieurs siècles</span>, <span lang="fr">Rome</span> was a <span lang="fr">république</span>, governed by <span lang="fr">un sénat</span> and two elected <span lang="fr">consuls</span>. Then, <span lang="fr">en</span> 27 <span lang="fr">avant J.-C.</span>, <span lang="fr">Octavien</span> — better known as <span lang="it">Augusto</span> — became <span lang="fr">le premier empereur</span>, and <span lang="fr">la république</span> transformed into <span lang="fr">un empire</span>. At its greatest extent, this <span lang="fr">empire</span> stretched from <span lang="fr">la Grande-Bretagne</span> to <span lang="it">l'Egitto</span>, from <span lang="fr">l'Espagne</span> to <span lang="fr">la Syrie</span>. <span lang="it">La lingua</span> of <span lang="fr">l'empire</span> was <span lang="fr">le latin</span>, and from <span lang="fr">le latin</span> would eventually descend <span lang="it">l'italiano</span>, <span lang="fr">le français</span>, <span lang="fr">l'espagnol</span>, and <span lang="fr">les autres langues romanes</span>.

### Paragraph 2 — Daily life and the fall

<span lang="it">La vita quotidiana</span> <span lang="fr">dans l'Empire romain</span> was remarkably <span lang="fr">sophistiquée pour son époque</span>. <span lang="fr">Les Romains</span> built <span lang="it">grandi strade</span> (<span lang="fr">les grandes routes</span>) that connected <span lang="fr">toutes les provinces</span> of <span lang="fr">l'empire</span> — <span lang="it">"tutte le strade portano a Roma,"</span> as the famous <span lang="fr">proverbe</span> says. <span lang="fr">Ils construisaient aussi</span> <span lang="it">acquedotti</span> (<span lang="fr">les aqueducs</span>) to bring <span lang="it">l'acqua</span> to <span lang="fr">les villes</span>, <span lang="fr">des thermes publics</span> where <span lang="it">i cittadini</span> could bathe and socialize, <span lang="fr">et de grands amphithéâtres</span> like <span lang="it">il Colosseo</span> <span lang="fr">à Rome</span>, where <span lang="fr">les gladiateurs</span> fought before <span lang="fr">des milliers de spectateurs</span>. <span lang="it">La famiglia romana</span> was governed by <span lang="fr">le pater familias</span>, <span lang="fr">le père</span>, who had <span lang="fr">une autorité</span> almost absolute over <span lang="fr">sa femme</span>, <span lang="fr">ses enfants</span>, <span lang="fr">et ses</span> <span lang="it">schiavi</span> (<span lang="fr">les esclaves</span>). <span lang="fr">Après</span> <span lang="it">quasi cinque secoli</span>, <span lang="fr">l'empire d'Occident</span> fell <span lang="fr">en</span> 476 <span lang="fr">après J.-C.</span>, but <span lang="fr">son héritage</span> — <span lang="fr">le droit</span>, <span lang="it">la lingua</span>, <span lang="fr">l'architecture</span>, <span lang="it">la religione</span> — <span lang="fr">continue de vivre</span> <span lang="it">in Italia</span> <span lang="fr">et partout en Europe</span>.

## Lesson 002 — From Fragmentation to Unification

**Level:** A2–B1
**Italian share:** ≈ 55% in both paragraphs
**Cognate patterns showcased:** `fr -tion / it -zione`, `fr -té / it -tà`, `fr -aire / it -ario`, `fr -ée / it -ata` (in past participles)
**Grammar features:** passato prossimo with both *essere* and *avere* auxiliaries (close parallel to French *être/avoir*); imperfetto in background descriptions; one receptive congiuntivo presente after *perché/pour que*; past-participle agreement with *essere*.

### Paragraph 3 — Medieval fragmentation and the Renaissance

<span lang="fr">Après la chute de</span> <span lang="it">l'Impero romano d'Occidente</span>, <span lang="it">l'Italia è entrata</span> <span lang="fr">dans une longue période</span> <span lang="fr">de</span> fragmentation. <span lang="fr">Pendant</span> <span lang="it">il Medioevo</span>, <span lang="fr">la péninsule</span> <span lang="it">non era</span> <span lang="fr">un pays unifié</span> — <span lang="it">era divisa</span> <span lang="fr">en</span> <span lang="it">molti piccoli stati</span>: <span lang="fr">des duchés, des républiques maritimes comme</span> <span lang="it">Venezia</span> <span lang="fr">et</span> <span lang="it">Genova</span>, <span lang="fr">des États pontificaux au centre, et</span> <span lang="it">il Regno di Napoli</span> <span lang="fr">dans le sud</span>. <span lang="it">Queste città-stato hanno sviluppato</span> <span lang="fr">des cultures distinctes, et certaines</span> <span lang="it">sono diventate</span> <span lang="fr">extrêmement riches grâce au commerce méditerranéen</span>. <span lang="it">Firenze</span>, <span lang="fr">par exemple</span>, <span lang="it">è diventata</span> <span lang="fr">le berceau de</span> <span lang="it">il Rinascimento</span> <span lang="fr">au quinzième siècle</span>, <span lang="fr">quand des familles comme</span> <span lang="it">i Medici hanno finanziato</span> <span lang="fr">des artistes comme</span> Leonardo da Vinci, Michelangelo, <span lang="fr">et</span> Raffaello. <span lang="it">La lingua italiana moderna nasce</span> <span lang="fr">précisément à cette époque</span>, <span lang="fr">principalement du dialecte toscan</span> <span lang="fr">que</span> Dante <span lang="fr">avait utilisé pour écrire</span> <span lang="it">la Divina Commedia</span>.

### Paragraph 4 — The Risorgimento and unification

<span lang="fr">Mais</span> <span lang="it">l'unità politica dell'Italia</span> <span lang="fr">n'arrive que beaucoup plus tard</span>. <span lang="it">Per quasi mille anni</span>, <span lang="fr">la ## Notes on Rendering

**In a plain Markdown viewer (GitHub, most editors):** the `<span lang="…">` tags are invisible but harmless, and the text reads as English with foreign-language interruptions. The languages blur together visually, which is not ideal for study but is fine for review and editing.

**In the project HTML with `assets/styles.css`:** French appears in deep blue italic and Italian in deep green bold italic, making the three-language structure immediately legible.

**With the planned `toggle.js`:** readers will be able to hide French to test Italian-only comprehension, hide Italian to see the scaffolding alone, or hover over any foreign span to reveal a gloss from `vocab.json`.
